# Does regular aerobic exercise causally reduce the risk of clinical depression?

Regular aerobic exercise is associated with a substantially lower incidence of clinical
depression across prospective cohorts [1]. A 2019 Mendelian-randomization analysis using
accelerometer-derived activity found evidence consistent with a *causal* protective effect,
estimating roughly a 26% lower odds of depression per standard-deviation increase in
objectively measured activity [2]. Randomized exercise-intervention trials in already-depressed
populations report moderate symptom reductions versus controls [3], supporting direction of
effect beyond reverse causation. The causal claim is strongest for moderate-intensity aerobic
activity; evidence for resistance-only training is thinner [1].
